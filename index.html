✅ You're Prepared

You've already served your Section 21 notice. Make sure it remains valid and court proceedings (if needed) must start by 31 July 2026.

What Happens After May 1, 2026?

  • Your existing Section 21 notice remains valid if served before deadline
  • You cannot serve NEW Section 21 notices
  • All evictions must use Section 8 grounds
  • You must join a landlord ombudsman scheme

👍 No Immediate Action Needed

Without tenants, you don't need to worry about Section 21 right now. But be aware of changes coming in May 2026.

Coming Changes

  • Section 21 abolished from May 1, 2026
  • All new tenancies will use periodic contracts
  • Must join landlord ombudsman scheme
  • Must provide government information sheet to tenants

🏛️ Key Changes in the Renters' Rights Act 2026

  • Last day to serve S21 - 4:30pm on 30 April 2026 (no exceptions). Acorn renters' union reports no-fault evictions rose from 1 in 5 (October 2025) to nearly 1 in 3 (January 2026) as landlords rush to evict before the ban (The Guardian, April 2026)
  • Court proceedings backstop - Any S21 served before 1 May: court action must start by 31 July 2026, or the notice lapses. Note: county court possession cases currently average 50+ weeks. The government is investing £50M to modernise and digitise civil courts, plus £5M/year for housing legal aid fee uplifts (gov.uk, April 2026)
  • Section 8 strengthened - New and improved grounds for possession
  • Periodic tenancies - All tenancies become periodic (no fixed terms). Break clauses in existing tenancy agreements will no longer be enforceable after 1 May 2026 — if you intended to use a break clause, it must be exercised before that date (Section 1 RRA)
  • Information Sheet - Must provide government Information Sheet to existing tenants by 31 May 2026 (£7,000 fine, up to £40,000 for repeated breaches)
  • Landlord ombudsman - Mandatory membership required
  • Decent Homes Standard - Applies to private rentals
  • Rent increases - New rules and limitations
